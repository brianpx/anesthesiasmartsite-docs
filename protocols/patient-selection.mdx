## Overview

Patient selection is critical to the success and safety of awake spine surgery. This page provides structured criteria for identifying appropriate candidates, screening for contraindications, and documenting the selection rationale.

<Callout kind="warning">
  Patient selection criteria should be applied in conjunction with your institutional policies and the specific surgical requirements of each case. Final determination of patient suitability is a clinical judgment.
</Callout>

## Primary Selection Criteria

| Criterion | Requirement | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| ASA Physical Status | I-II (III case-by-case) | Lower perioperative risk with MAC; ASA III patients require additional risk-benefit analysis |
| Psychological Suitability | Passes anxiety screening | Patient must tolerate awake positioning and procedure without excessive distress |
| BMI | Typically less than 30 kg/m2 | Positioning challenges and airway management complexity increase with higher BMI |
| Airway Assessment | No anticipated difficult airway | Must be able to convert to general anesthesia rapidly if needed |
| Surgical Duration | Within institutional MAC time limits | Prolonged procedures increase patient discomfort and complication risk |

## ASA Physical Status Assessment

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="ASA I" icon="check-circle">
    **Appropriate candidate.** Healthy patient with no significant medical conditions.

    - Standard MAC protocol applies
    - Standard monitoring sufficient
    - Low risk for conversion to general anesthesia
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="ASA II" icon="check-circle">
    **Appropriate candidate.** Patient with mild systemic disease that does not limit activity.

    - Review specific comorbidities for interaction with sedation agents
    - Assess medication list for potential interactions with dexmedetomidine
    - Consider baseline vital signs (e.g., resting bradycardia with dexmedetomidine use)
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="ASA III" icon="alert-triangle">
    **Case-by-case evaluation.** Patient with severe systemic disease that limits activity.

    - Requires individual risk-benefit analysis by the anesthesiologist and surgeon
    - Consider whether the benefits of awake technique outweigh the risks in this patient
    - Document the rationale for proceeding or declining
    - Additional monitoring may be required
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Psychological Screening

Patients undergoing awake procedures must be psychologically prepared for the experience. Screen for:

- **Anxiety level** — Moderate to severe anxiety is a relative contraindication; assess using validated tools or clinical interview
- **Claustrophobia** — Prone positioning with surgical drapes can trigger claustrophobic responses
- **Prior surgical experience** — Patients with negative prior experiences under sedation may require additional counseling
- **Cognitive function** — Patient must be able to follow commands for neurological assessment
- **Language and communication** — Ensure the patient and care team can communicate effectively during the procedure

<Callout kind="tip">
  Pre-operative counseling significantly improves patient tolerance of awake procedures. Explain what the patient will experience — sounds, sensations, positioning, communication methods — and address concerns before the day of surgery.
</Callout>

## BMI Considerations

BMI guidelines serve as a practical threshold based on positioning and airway management factors:

| BMI Range | Consideration |
|-----------|---------------|
| Less than 25 | Standard candidate — no additional concerns related to body habitus |
| 25-30 | Acceptable with attention to positioning comfort and airway access |
| 30-35 | Relative caution — assess prone positioning feasibility and airway plan |
| Greater than 35 | Generally not recommended — increased risk of positioning complications and difficult airway |

<Callout kind="info">
  BMI thresholds are guidelines, not absolute cutoffs. Individual body habitus, fat distribution, and the specific surgical position required should inform the final assessment.
</Callout>

## Contraindications

### Absolute Contraindications

- Patient refusal after informed consent discussion
- Inability to communicate during the procedure (language barrier without interpreter, cognitive impairment)
- Known allergy to planned sedation or local anesthetic agents
- Conditions requiring general anesthesia (e.g., need for muscle relaxation, hemodynamic instability)

### Relative Contraindications

- Severe anxiety disorder despite counseling and anxiolytic premedication
- Obstructive sleep apnea (increased sensitivity to sedation, airway concerns in prone position)
- Anticipated difficult airway (limits ability to convert to general anesthesia)
- Chronic pain requiring high baseline opioid doses (difficult to achieve adequate analgesia with MAC)
- Coagulopathy (risk consideration for local anesthetic injection)

## Documentation

Document the selection assessment in the pre-operative evaluation:

1. ASA classification with supporting rationale
2. Psychological screening results and patient's stated willingness to proceed
3. BMI and body habitus assessment relevant to positioning
4. Airway assessment and conversion plan
5. Any relative contraindications identified and rationale for proceeding
